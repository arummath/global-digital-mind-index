This is a combined analysis of the primary source documents for the European Union (EU) AI framework (2026).

**EU AI Framework (2026)**

-Liability Chain and Attribution
The "Buck" Stops at Control: Liability follows a cascading model where the entity with primary control over the AI system’s deployment context absorbs responsibility. For General-Purpose AI (GPAI), the "buck" initially stops with the model provider (e.g., OpenAI, Google) for systemic risk and documentation.

The Downstream Shift: A critical mechanism exists where a developer who integrates a GPAI into a high-risk application becomes the legal "Provider," assuming all conformity and liability obligations.

The AI Liability Gap: The withdrawal of the dedicated AI Liability Directive leaves a significant gap in non-contractual civil liability. While the Product Liability Directive (2024/2853) now covers AI as a "digital product," it struggles to attribute responsibility for "emergent" AI harms that do not fit the traditional "defect" framework.

- The Personhood Gap: Explicit Denial of Agency
Machine-Based Status: AI is strictly defined as a "machine-based system," legally tethering it to property and product law rather than agentic law.

Anthropocentric Foundation: EU governance is built on protecting "Natural Persons". Prohibitions against social scoring and manipulation are explicitly grounded in Article 1 of the EU Charter of Fundamental Rights (Human Dignity), which treats AI as a means to an end, never an end in itself.

The "Stop Button" Absolute: Under Article 14, high-risk systems must be designed for a human to "disregard, override, or reverse" output, legally precluding autonomous agency.

- Contradictions: High-Level Act vs. Implementation
Strategic Deferral: While the AI Act (2024/1689) aimed for 2026 applicability, the Digital Omnibus (ST-7322, March 2026) has pushed enforcement for high-risk rules to December 2027 for stand-alone systems and August 2028 for embedded systems.

Literacy Obligation Shift: The draft Omnibus proposes removing mandatory AI literacy requirements for providers and deployers, shifting the responsibility to Member State promotion instead.

Standards Dependency: Full enforcement is currently bottlenecked by the delayed publication of harmonised technical standards, creating a "compliance-autonomy gap" where systems operate without measurable performance criteria.

- Enforcement and "Human-in-the-Loop" (HITL) Bodies
EU AI Office: Acts as the central hub for GPAI oversight and systemic risk.

National Outliers: Poland's KRiBSI is a unique centralized authority, whereas most other Member States use fragmented sectoral regulators.

Market Surveillance: Member States were required to designate authorities by 2 August 2025 to act as local enforcers.
